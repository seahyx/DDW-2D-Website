Methods

How did we select, refine, model, and analyse our data?

Country Selection

To begin selecting countries, we chose countries that had a similar GDP per capita. By keeping the GDP of these countries similar it allows us to isolate it and choose other factors better. Initially starting with 10 countries, we eventually scaled down to the following 6 countries as they had the most robust datasets to work with: Morocco, Algeria, Egypt, Nigeria, Namibia, and South Africa. For geographical context, the relevant countries we selected are located at the following areas of the African continent.

Predictor Variables

We initially started out by using the following variables as predictor variables.

Agricultural Land Use per Capita
This includes 3 specific categories: temporary crops (rice, wheat), temporary meadows and permanent meadows (meadows - livestock grazing and animal rearing). These metrics represent the local food production - we hypothesise that an increase in this metric would increase local food availibility and thus decrease the PoU. Furthermore, it reflects the possible dependency on food imports, which are likely to be priced higher which would cause PoU as a result of lower income and lower food affordability.
Share of Employment in Agriculture
Employment in agriculture includes all agricultural divisions in the country - which include agriculture, forestry and fishing. We hypothesise that a greater proportion of people working in agriculture would increase local food production and availibility, reducing the cost of food and thus decrease PoU. 
Government Expenditure on Agriculture per Capita
Government expenditure is categorised into recurrent and capital expenditure into all of the agricultural divisions (mentioned in above cell). Recurrent expenditure focuses on standard operations while capital expenditure focuses on business growth and other improvements. We took the total of these two as both are needed for a high-functioning agriculture industry. Key assumptions include: efficacious and productive use of the funds, zero corruption in managing and distributing the funds and reliable FAO data - countries did not over or under-report spending. Our hypothesis is that government subsidies decrease the costs faced by the farmer and farming companies, enabling them to sell their crops locally at a lower price, helping decrease PoU.
Amount of Imorted Staple Food per Capita
This includes the total imported rice and wheat in tons by a given country per year. We assume that there is no oversupply of staple foods in the country (whether it be due to over-importing or excess local production) which leads to the market being at equilibrium and food prices are not unreasonably low. We hypotheise that higher food imports would increase food prices and thus increase PoU.
Consumer Price Indices
This measures the inflation in food prices. We hypothesise that high inflation would cause resident income to not be able to keep up and lead to greater PoU.
GDP per Capita
This measures the average income of citizens. We hypothesise that greater average income would enable citizens to buy (and consume) more food, ceteris paribus, and thus decrease PoU.

Predictor Variable Selection

In order to select good predictor variables out of all of these, we generated a pairplot and a correlation table, plotted against all independent variables to evaluate the correlation between each other. Variables with low correlation and pattern were then chosen in order to obtain the most optimal model.

As can be seen from the pairplot and correlation table above, food imports, GDP per capita, expenditure per capita, and CPI has the lowest correlation between each other. The majority of the other independent variables are not well-spread out or are highly correlated with each other. Variables that have distributions closer to a normal distribution are preferred, namely GDP per capita and CPI. Thus, the three variables that are chosen are Food Balance per capita, GDP per capita, and CPI.

Training, First Iteration

We used a linear regression model in our first training.

For our first iteration, we normalized the data after training split, which gave us the RMSE spread of as shown above.

As can be visualized from the boxplots above, the linear model is the most reliable and accurate. It has the least spread and lowest mean and median.

However, there is still considerable spread and outliers in the median RMSE of the linear model, even though the RMSE is not supposed to change by a large amount through different random seeds for training/test splits. This shows that our model is still sensitive to small changes in the training/test split and its reliability can be improved.

Training, Second Iteration

In the second iteration, the feature dataset is normalized before the training/test split. This model has managed to achieve a much better performance than the first iteration.

Again, it can be visualized from the boxplots above that the linear model is the most reliable and accurate. It has the least spread and the lowest mean and median. As comparison, the plot from the first iteration is shown beside the plot for the second iteration. It is very apparent that the improved second iteration has greatly improved the reliability of the models, attributing to the minimized spread of the RMSE of the model across the linear, quadratic, and cubic models.

Notably, the linear model has gained the most improvements from the changes with its spread and outliers being significantly smaller than that of the second iteration. Additionally, the model is now much more accurate, with its mean and median being much closer to 0 (where 0 is perfect accuracy).

Thus, it is clear that the linear model of the second iteration is the most accurate and reliable model, and we will thus be choosing it as our final model.
